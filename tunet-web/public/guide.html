① The big idea

What does Spark Flint actually do?

It learns a single, repeatable image fix from your examples — then applies that fix to every other frame for you.

Think of it as an apprentice retoucher. You sit down and fix the first 30 frames of a shot by hand — remove a wire, clean up skin, whatever. The apprentice watches every "before → after" you do. Once it's seen enough, it can finish the remaining 970 frames in the same style, on its own. Spark Flint is that apprentice — and the "watching" part is what we call training.

Under the hood it's a small neural network that does image-to-image mapping: feed it a frame, it hands back a fixed version. It doesn't "understand" your shot — it just gets very, very good at copying the specific change you demonstrated. That's why one trained model does one job well, rather than everything poorly.

What it's great for

When you pick "New Job", you start from a preset — a bundle of sensible settings tuned for a common VFX task. You can train from scratch with any of these:

✨ Beauty / Paint Fix

Best for small, local fixes — blemishes, wire/rig removal, skin retouch. It focuses hard on just the area you changed so tiny edits don't get washed out, and it's tuned to keep skin and texture looking real.

small fixesmost popularauto-mask

🪄 Paintout / Cleanup

Best for removing large objects and cleaning big areas. Tuned for smooth, even blends across the fill, and it skips the untouched parts of the frame so training time goes where the actual work is.

large areassmooth blends

🎭 Roto / Matte

Turn a plate into a black-and-white matte. You show it plate → matte pairs and it learns to pull the matte on new frames.

matteB/W output

🔧 General Image-to-Image

No masking — it treats the whole frame equally. Reach for it when the entire image changes (a grade, an AOV, a global look), rather than for small spot fixes.

whole-frameglobal looks

One model = one shot, one task. A model trained to remove a rig from this shot won't magically remove rigs from a different shot. That's normal — it's specialized on purpose. New shot? Train a new model (it's fast and cheap).

② How it's different

Spark Flint vs. CopyCat

If you've used Nuke's CopyCat, you already get the concept. Spark Flint is the same idea — show examples, get a model — with a lot more under the hood, and it doesn't tie up your workstation.

Foundry's CopyCat pioneered "train-by-example" right inside Nuke, and it's excellent. Spark Flint takes that same workflow and runs it on rented cloud GPUs, adds VFX-specific presets and live coaching, and — crucially — exports back to Nuke and Flame and After Effects, so you're not locked to one app.

③ Creating a dataset

Building your before/after pairs

A dataset is just two folders of matching frames: the "before" and the "after". Get this right and everything else is easy.

It's a stack of flashcards. Front of the card = the messy frame (Source). Back of the card = how you want it to look (Target). The model studies the stack until it can predict the back from the front.

The two folders

📁 Source (the "before")

Your original frames — the ones with the problem. Folder can be named src, source, in, or input.

📁 Target (the "after")

The same frames, fixed by hand. Folder can be named dst, dest, out, output, or target.

Filenames must match across the two folders. frame_001.exr in Source needs a frame_001.exr in Target. That's how the model knows which "after" goes with which "before". Mismatched names = that pair is skipped.

How many frames?

You don't need many — 20–50 well-chosen pairs is plenty for most shots. What matters more than quantity is variety: include frames that cover the range of motion, lighting, and angles in your shot. If the apprentice only ever sees the subject facing left, it'll struggle when they turn right.

Optional: a validation set

You can also add val_src / val_dst folders with a few held-out frames the model never trains on. These are the model's "pop quiz" — a fair test of whether it actually learned the task or just memorized the study set. Totally optional, but nice for honest previews.

Folder layout, all together

my_shot/
├─ src/        # before frames  → frame_001.exr, frame_002.exr …
├─ dst/        # after frames   → frame_001.exr, frame_002.exr … (names match!)
├─ val_src/   # optional held-out "before" frames
└─ val_dst/   # optional held-out "after" frames

Supported formats: PNG, JPG/JPEG, EXR, TIF/TIFF, BMP, WEBP. EXR keeps your scene-linear data intact.

Always "Review pairs" before you submit. Spark Flint makes you eyeball every Source ↔ Target ↔ Diff before a paid run can start. Thirty seconds here catches the classic disaster — a misaligned or wrong-folder dataset — before it costs you a single cent of GPU time.

④ Submitting a job

Starting a training run

Five short steps on the "New Job" page. The form fills in smart defaults — you mostly just confirm.

Pick a mode

New trains from scratch (the usual choice). Resume continues an earlier run where it left off. Fine-tune starts from an existing model's weights — handy when a similar shot already has a good model.

Name it

A friendly label like hero_beauty_v3. This becomes the output folder name and the title you'll see on the dashboard. Convention: <project>_<task>_<version>.

Point it at your data

Pick your project folder and Spark Flint auto-detects the src/ and dst/ subfolders and counts your pairs. Then hit Review pairs to approve them. (You'll upload the frames to storage; the form shows you the path.)

Choose a preset & a GPU

Pick the preset that matches your task (Beauty, Paintout, Roto, General). Then pick a GPU — bigger/faster costs more per hour but finishes sooner. The form shows a live cost & time estimate based on your frame count and settings.

Submit

That's it. The job provisions a GPU, starts training, and your dashboard goes live with the loss curve and preview updating every few seconds.

You don't have to set an end time. By default a run trains until you stop it — and the live analysis (next section) tells you exactly when that should be. You can also set a step limit for hands-off overnight runs, and turn on email alerts so you're pinged when it plateaus instead of babysitting.

⑤ Reading the numbers

Steps, epochs, loss & the curve

Four words show up everywhere once training starts. Here's what each one means — no math required.

👣 Step

One practice rep. The model looks at a small crop, guesses the fix, checks it against your "after", and nudges itself to do better. Thousands of these add up.

📚 Epoch

One full lap through the study material. By default that's 500 steps. "Epoch 12" just means it's on its 12th pass — a friendly progress counter.

🎯 Loss

How wrong the model currently is — like a golf score. It measures the gap between the model's guess and your real "after". Lower is better.

📉 Training curve

Loss plotted over time. A healthy run drops fast, then flattens. The shape tells you almost everything about how it's going.

What a healthy training curve looks like

  • Steep drop at the start — the model is learning the obvious stuff fast. This is normal and exciting.
  • The knee / flattening — easy wins are done; now it's polishing fine detail.
  • The flat plateau — it's basically as good as it'll get with this data. More training here mostly just costs money.
  • The faint dots are the raw, noisy per-epoch loss; the bold line is the smoothed trend. Watch the line, not the jitter.

If the curve goes up and stays up, that's a red flag. Usually it means the learning rate is too high, or some of your pairs are mismatched. Stop, fix it, and restart — don't let it cook.

The other stats, quickly

Beside the curve you'll see a strip of numbers. The ones worth knowing:

  • Loss Best (green) — the lowest loss the model has hit so far, and which epoch it happened. Your real "high score".
  • Val Loss — the score on the held-out pop-quiz frames. If this stops dropping while training loss keeps falling, the model is starting to memorize rather than learn.
  • PSNR / SSIM / LPIPS — different rulers for "how close to the target?" Higher PSNR/SSIM = closer; lower LPIPS = looks more realistic to a human eye. You don't need to chase these; the preview is the real judge.
  • Cost / Epoch — a live running estimate of what each lap is costing you. Great for deciding when "good enough" is good enough.
⑥ Training analysis

The "should I stop?" panel

Reading curves is a skill. So Spark Flint reads it for you and posts a plain-language verdict. These four chips are your dashboard's coach.

TrendImproving
Recent change−4.2%
PlateauBest 12ep ago
StatusTraining well

What each chip means

Trend

The big-picture direction of the smoothed loss over the recent window.

  • Improving — loss is still falling. Keep going.
  • Flat — barely moving. It may be done, or just resting between gains.
  • Diverging — loss is climbing. Something's wrong (see below).
Recent Change

How much loss moved in the latest window, as a percentage. A negative number is good — it means loss dropped. −4.2% = "4% better recently."

Plateau

How long ago the model hit its best score. "Best 12ep ago" means it set a new record 12 epochs back. The longer this gets, the more likely it's finished.

  • under 10 epochs — fresh progress, all good (green).
  • 10–30 epochs — slowing down, keep an eye on it (amber).
  • 30+ epochs — probably as good as it gets (red).
Statusthe one to act on

The bottom-line recommendation that blends the other three into a single call. If you only read one chip, read this one.

The verdicts, and what to do

Training well Loss is dropping (or a new best just landed). Let it keep running.
Slowing — keep watching Gains are getting small. Glance at the preview — if it already looks right, you can stop and save money.
Plateau — may stop No new best in ~30 epochs. Usually done. Check the preview, then stop if it looks good.
Stop — no new best in 50+ epochs It's coasting. Further training is mostly wasted spend. Stop it.
Stop — diverging, lower LR Loss is rising. Stop, lower the learning rate (or check for bad pairs), and restart.

The numbers advise; the preview decides. A "plateau" with a preview that already looks perfect means you're done — ship it. The analysis exists so you stop paying for a model that finished ten minutes ago. Turn on email alerts to get pinged on plateau/diverge without watching the screen.

⑦ Reading the preview

The preview grid, decoded

Every few seconds the model shows its homework: a strip of columns comparing what it was given, what you wanted, and what it produced. This is the most honest signal of all.

Each row is one sample frame (it shows up to three). The columns, left to right, are always in this order:

SOURCEthe "before"
TARGETyour "after"
PREDICTIONmodel's attempt
DIFFwhere they differ
training_preview.jpg — one row per sample, refreshed every ~5s while live
1

Source

What the model was handed — your unedited frame.

2

Target

The answer — your hand-fixed frame. This is the goal it's aiming for.

3

Prediction

The model's current attempt. When this matches column 2, you're done.

4

Diff

A heatmap of the gap between Prediction and Target. Hot = still wrong, dark = matching.

How to read it

  • Early on: the Prediction looks mushy or just like the Source, and the Diff is bright. Normal — it hasn't learned yet.
  • Going well: the Prediction creeps toward the Target and the Diff goes dark and cool. A near-black Diff is the picture of success.
  • Done: when you genuinely can't tell Prediction from Target, stop training. That's the finish line — trust your eyes over any number.

Extra columns you might see

🎯 Auto-mask column

If auto-mask is on (Beauty/Paintout presets), an extra column shows the white-on-black map of which pixels the model is told to care about — the area that changed between your before and after.

🖊️ Mask column

If you supplied your own masks, they appear as a column too, right after Source — so you can confirm the right region is being emphasized.

Training vs. Validation tabs

The preview has two tabs. Training uses frames the model studies from. Validation uses the held-out pop-quiz frames it has never trained on — the more honest test. If Validation looks as good as Training, your model truly generalizes. You can also expand any preview to full-screen and zoom/pan to pixel-peep.

⑧ Advanced settings

Every dial, in plain English

Here's the honest truth: a preset already sets all of these well. You can train great models without opening a single group below. They're here for when you want more control.

Rule of thumb: change one thing at a time, and only if a preset isn't getting you there. If you're not sure — leave it. The defaults are the result of a lot of real runs. Tap a section to expand it.

Model & image qualityHow big the model is and how much detail it sees
model_typeleave it

The model's architecture (its "brain shape").

  • unet — fast, general-purpose.
  • msrn — sharper fine detail, trains a bit slower.
model_size_dims128 is the sweet spot

How much "brainpower" the model has. Bigger isn't always better — it just costs more VRAM and time.

  • 64 lightweight · 128 balanced · 256 higher quality · 512 maximum (lots of VRAM).
resolution512

The size of crop the model studies each step — like a magnifying glass sliding over your frame. Bigger sees more context but is much slower (cost rises with the square of the size).

  • 256 fast, small fixes · 512 great default · 768–1024 big blends, heavy VRAM.
overlap_factor0.25–0.5

How much neighbouring crops overlap as they tile across the frame. More overlap = smoother, seam-free blends but more training time.

  • 0.0 fastest, risks seams · 0.25 balanced · 0.5 sharper edges · 0.75 seamless big paint fixes.
color_spaceauto

Must match your footage. srgb for normal 8-bit images (PNG/JPG); linear for scene-linear 32-bit EXR. Wrong choice = blown highlights or muddy shadows. Leave on auto and it picks from your file type.

Learning behaviourHow the model measures mistakes and how fast it adapts
lossset by preset

How the model scores its own mistakes — its definition of "wrong".

  • l1 — pixel difference. Sharp, stable, best all-rounder.
  • l2 — smoother, can blur fine detail.
  • l1+lpips — adds a "looks real to a human" score. Best for skin/beauty.
  • weighted — a custom blend of the above (power users).
  • bce+dice — only for roto/matte (black-and-white output).
lr (learning rate)1e-4

How big a step the model takes each time it corrects itself. Too small = slow; too big = unstable.

  • Loss falling too slowly? Try a little faster (3e-4).
  • Loss spiking up and never recovering? Your LR is too high — go slower (5e-5).
lr_schedulernone

Whether the learning rate changes over time. none keeps it constant (simple, good); cosine eases it down over long runs; plateau drops it automatically when loss stalls.

lambda_lpips0.1–0.3

Only active with l1+lpips. How much the model prioritises "looking right" over matching exact pixels. Higher = sharper/more realistic but can drift; above 0.5 risks artifacts.

l1_weight · l2_weight · lpips_weight

The individual ingredient amounts when loss = weighted. A blend of L1 (sharp) + a little L2 (smooth) + a little LPIPS (realistic). Power-user territory.

use_ampon

Trains roughly 2× faster using less GPU memory, with almost no quality cost. Leave it on. (Only turn off in the very rare case of NaN losses.)

Length & schedulingHow long it runs and how big each batch is
batch_sizeAuto

How many crops the model studies per step. Bigger = steadier learning but more VRAM. Auto probes your GPU and picks the largest that fits. Drop to 2 if you ever hit out-of-memory.

iterations_per_epoch500

How many steps make one "lap" before it saves a checkpoint and runs validation. Lower = saves more often (nice for watching); higher = slightly faster overall.

max_steps0 = run until you stop

An optional auto-stop limit. Leave at 0 to train until you (or the analysis) decide to stop. Set a number for hands-off overnight or queued runs.

progressive_resolution

Start at low resolution to learn shapes fast, then scale up to full detail. Speeds up early training — recommended for roto/matte work.

Focus & masksTell the model which part of the frame matters
use_auto_maskon for Beauty/Paintout

Automatically builds a mask by comparing your before/after — changed pixels become "important", identical pixels are ignored. No manual mask painting needed. Great for beauty, paint, cleanup.

auto_mask_gamma0.5–1.0

Adjusts how generously the auto-mask spreads. Below 1.0 grows the white area (good for subtle beauty work); above 1.0 tightens it; 1.0 leaves it as-is.

use_mask_loss + mask_weightweight 100 for tiny fixes

Makes masked (white) areas count more toward the score, so the model can't "win" by just copying the untouched background. Essential for small fixes — a lipstick smudge that's 0.3% of frame needs a high weight (e.g. 100) or the model ignores it.

use_mask_inputadvanced

Feeds the mask to the model as an extra channel alongside RGB, so it can treat masked/unmasked areas differently. Note: this changes the model's shape — you can't toggle it mid-training.

skip_empty_patches + threshold

Skips crops where the before and after are identical, so training time is spent only where there's an actual change to learn. Needs auto-mask on. The threshold sets how different a pixel must be to "count".

AugmentationsShow the model extra variety so it generalizes

Augmentations randomly tweak your training frames (flip, nudge, re-light) so the model sees more variety than you actually shot — like quizzing the apprentice with the same flashcards held at different angles. The same tweak is applied to both Source and Target so pairs stay aligned.

aug_hflip

Randomly mirror frames left-to-right. Good for most tasks; skip it for text or anything direction-specific.

aug_affine (+ keep_ratio)

Random scale / shift / rotate / shear. Helps the model cope with slight alignment differences. Keep aspect ratio on so paired frames stay registered.

aug_gamma

Randomly shifts the brightness curve so the model handles varying exposure.

aug_color (brightness · contrast · saturation)

Randomly nudges color and tone. Use it when your dataset has varied lighting or grades; skip it when your footage is already well-matched or color-critical.

Saving, previews & alertsHow often it checkpoints, previews, and pings you
preview_batch_interval50

Save a fresh preview image every N steps. Lower = see progress sooner (each preview costs a little GPU time). 0 disables previews.

preview_refresh_rate

How many preview saves between panel refreshes — keeps the UI snappy during heavy training.

log_interval5

How often the loss number is printed to the log. Lower = noisier log, higher = cleaner.

keep_last_checkpoints4

How many older saved models to keep on disk (plus the newest). More = more rollback options, more storage used.

early stopping (enable · patience · stop)

A safety net. When loss stops improving for patience epochs (default 30), it saves a "best so far" checkpoint. Leave stop off to keep training but bank the best model; turn it on to halt automatically.

Email alerts

Optionally get an email when the run plateaus/finishes or starts diverging, so you can stop it without watching the dashboard. One email per kind, with a cooldown so you're not spammed.

Exporting your modelGet a file you can drop into Nuke, Flame or After Effects
auto_export_intervalevery 10 epochs

Export the model automatically every N epochs during training, so a ready-to-use file appears in your Downloads panel without stopping the run. 0 disables it.

auto_export_flame (ONNX)on

Exports an ONNX model that drops straight into Autodesk Flame's ML node and Adobe After Effects (e.g. Cleanplate FX).

auto_export_nuke (.cat)

Exports a TorchScript model plus a Nuke helper script. Run the helper in Nuke to produce the .cat file for the Inference node — exactly like a CopyCat result.

finetune_from

Start a new run from an existing model's weights instead of from scratch — useful when a similar shot already trained well. Only the weights load; the optimizer resets.

That's the whole tool. Pick a preset, show it good before/after pairs, watch the preview until Prediction matches Target, then stop and export. Everything above is just the "why" behind the buttons. Now go train something. ✦
